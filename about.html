Tools built for teaching,
not for software.

A note on what this site is, how it was made, and why it looks the way it does.

Why this exists

After enough years of teaching, the weight of repetitive admin, mediocre platform tools, and bloated edtech systems becomes genuinely exhausting. A lot of what passes for "teaching technology" adds friction rather than removing it. Lesson plan templates too long to complete in a planning period. Trackers that require more maintenance than the teaching itself. Platforms that cost too much and do too little of what you actually need in a classroom.

Most of it is also designed for the buyer — a department head, an IT coordinator, a procurement decision — not for the teacher who will use it daily. The result is software that performs capability rather than delivering it. Feature lists over usefulness. Dashboards over clarity.

Free Range Tutors is an attempt to build something different: small tools, specific resources, and things that solve one problem well and stay out of the way. Everything here was built because it was needed and a decent free version didn't exist. The goal is lower mental fatigue during planning and admin, not higher productivity metrics.

How things are designed here

The visual language of this site is deliberately document-like. It does not try to look like a SaaS product. There are no dashboards, no onboarding flows, no progress rings. The interface is meant to recede — to feel closer to a well-organised worksheet than an application.

This is a considered choice. Teachers already carry a significant cognitive load. The tools and resources here should not add to it. A calm, readable layout with clear hierarchy and no unnecessary animation is more useful than a visually impressive one that requires attention to navigate.

No tool on this site uses a fixed sidebar, a sticky header, or a modal-heavy interaction pattern. Everything scrolls as a single document. The interfaces behave predictably on any device, at any screen size, including when projected onto a classroom display mid-lesson.

Low friction

Every tool should be usable immediately, without setup, account creation, or a tutorial. If it needs explaining, it needs simplifying.

Document-like UI

Interfaces should behave like well-designed documents, not applications. Scroll naturally. No fixed chrome. No persistent panels demanding attention.

Reduced cognitive load

Calm colours, legible type, and clear structure. The interface should be forgettable in the best sense — it gets out of the way so the teaching work can happen.

Classroom-first

Practical usefulness in a real lesson, with real students, takes priority over theoretical completeness or visual impressiveness.

Honest about state

Things in progress are labelled as such. Experimental tools say they are experimental. Nothing is dressed up beyond its actual maturity.

How to use this site

There is no intended order. Browse the tools and resources sections and take what is useful. Nothing requires registration or installation. Most tools save state locally in your browser if they need to persist anything — nothing is sent to a server.

The resources section contains printable and downloadable materials: speaking activities, field packs, task cards, and similar items. They are organised by type and level. Use them as-is, adapt them, or ignore the ones that do not suit your context. They are not prescriptive.

The tools section contains web apps that run entirely in the browser. Some are stable and well-tested. Some are marked as beta or experimental — meaning they work but may have rough edges, or are still being refined based on classroom use. The badges on each card are an honest indication of where a tool currently sits.

If something does not work as expected, the contact page is there. This is a small project and feedback goes directly to the person who built it.

This site is built and maintained by one teacher, outside of working hours. It is not a startup. It is not angled toward monetisation. The tools are free because they were built to solve a teaching problem, not a commercial one.

It is, by design, always unfinished. Teaching practice changes. Resources get revised. Tools get rebuilt when a better approach becomes clear. That is the intention.
